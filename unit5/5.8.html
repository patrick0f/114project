Lesson 5.8 Levels of Intellect

Levels of intelligence-

The intellectually disabled have an IQ of 70 or lower. High intelligence is 135 IQ and higher.


Intellectual disability-

Having significantly below average intellectual functioning and limitations in at least two areas of adaptive functioning.


Adaptive functioning includes communication skills, self-care, ability to be independent, social skills, community involvement, self-direction, health and safety, academics, and leisure and work.


Causes include genetics (down syndrome, those with an extra chromosome), environmental factors (fetal alcohol syndrome, sbs), deprivation or neglect, and other unknown causes.


There is no cure, but there are some types of preventative measures for intellectual disability. A test can be performed on newborns for a hidden genetic disorder called PKU, and if detected early, can be prevented with a special diet (no protein). General counseling, pregnancy care services, and education of new parents are other preventative strategies.


Levels of disability-

Those with mild disability have an IQ of 50-70 and encompass 85% of those disabled. Their academic abilities are at a 6th grade level, but they can learn to be independent and have a job.


Those with moderate disability have an IQ of 35-49 and have academic abilities at a second-grade level. They can be trained in self-care and acquire reading/writing skills but do require supervision.


Those with severe disability have an IQ of 20-34 and have the mental capacity of a 5 year old. They can learn to talk and perform simple tasks but require close supervision.


Those with profound disabilities have an IQ below 20 and a mental age of less than 3. They have limited communication and need constant supervision, typically with many physical disabilities.


Intellectually gifted-

Most research on giftedness was done by Lewis Terman, and the resulting data is much of what we know about the subject. He found that most gifted children excelled in school, had good health, and were happy overall. Most went on the path of success, but most lived an ordinary life.


Genetics and environment-

The most passionately followed question in psychology is if intelligence is due to genetics or the environment.


Studies of family members, twins, and adopted children support the idea that there is a significant genetic contribution to intelligence. Those raised together and more genetically related had higher correlated IQ.


Genetic influences-

James Bouchard came across two twins separated at birth that lived remarkably similar lives. He studied over 100 pairs of twins separated at birth that had similar personalities, temperament, intelligence, etc.


Adopted children have more similar IQ to their biological parents than their adoptive ones.


Research from the human genome project has also suggested intelligence has a genetic role. The genetic basis of intelligence is complicated, however, because it involves the interaction of many genes.


Schooling effects-

Increased schooling is related to higher intelligence scores.

Heritability-

The amount of trait variation within a group that can be attributed to genetic differences.


Heritability and heredity are not the same. Heritability looks at the differences in groups of people while heredity looks at inherited individual traits. Heritable differences are with a group sharing the same environment.


Heritability is highest in groups who share the same environment but have different genes.
