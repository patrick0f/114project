Lesson 5.10 Language

Language-

Our spoken, written, or gestured word; the way we communicate meaning to ourselves and others.


Phonemes-

The smallest distinctive sound unit in a spoken language. Bat has three phonemes, b,a,t. There are about 40 phonemes.


Morpheme-

The smallest unit that carries meaning that may be a word or part of a word. Unforgettable has 4 morphemes, un,for,get,table. There are 100,000 morphemes.


Words-

Meaningful units. There are about 290,500 words.


Phrase-

Composed of two or more words. There are about 326,000 phrases.


Sentence-

Composed of many words. There are an infinite number of sentences.


Grammar-

The system of rules in a language that enables communication and understanding. It is composed of semantics and syntax.


Semantics-

A set of rules through which meaning is derived from morphemes, words, and sentences. Semantic rules say that adding -ed (a morpheme) to the end of a word means it occurred in the past.


Syntax-

The rules for combining words into grammatically sensible sentences. Syntactical rule is that adjectives come before nouns, like white house


Language development-

Children learn their native language much before learning 2+2. After age 1, we learn 3,500 words a year, meaning we know 60,000 words by the time high school is over.


Babbling stage-

Beginning at 4 months, the infant spontaneously utters various sounds like goo-goo-ga-ga. Babbling isn’t an imitation of adult speech. Until babies are 9-10 months old, they can’t differentiate the native language of their household.


One-word stage-

On the first birthday or so, a child begins speaking one-word pieces to communicate. These words usually are short and begin with consonants (letters that aren’t vowels) like b, d , m, p, or t. Children can understand a lot of the language they hear at and before this stage.


Two-word stage-

Before their second year, children start speaking in two-word sentences.


Telegraphic speech-

The form of speech in which children speak is like a telegram, using mainly nouns and verbs. “Go car” means “I want to go in the car.


Longer phrases-

After telegraphic speech children will start uttering longer phrases, like “go to store”, with syntactical sense, and they start understanding humor by early elementary school.


Skinner-

Believed that we learn to talk through operant conditioning. He thought that language development is not inherently born with us, it can be explained on the basis of learning principles such as association, imitation, and reinforcement. Children learn to speak because they are rewarded for making sounds close to adult speech, they are shaped until they form correct words, such as with praise and reinforcement.


Chomsky-

Opposed Skinner’s ideas and suggested that the rate of language acquisition is so fast that it can’t be explained by learning. As a result, language is inborn. He supports his claim with the idea that languages have a universal grammar, a similar underlying structure of all languages. He believes in the presence of a language acquisition device that is switched on through exposure to language in the environment.


Statistical learning-

Before age 1, our brains analyze which syllables in words go together in words. This analysis is learned during critical periods of child development.


Benjamin Whorf-

Created the idea of linguistic determinism, language determines the way we think and perceive the world.


Thinking in language-

When a language provides words for objects or events, we can think about them more clearly and retain them. It is easier to think about two colors with two different names than two colors with the same name.


To a large extent thinking is language based, but we also think in images. We don’t think in words when riding a bicycle, for example.


When we imagine doing a physical activity, the same corresponding brain regions activate as when we actually perform that activity. Most psychologists believe thoughts influence language and vice versa.


Other Important Vocabulary

Iconic memory-

A momentary sensory memory of visual stimuli.


Long term potentiation-

An increase in a synapse’s firing potential after a brief rapid stimulation.


Rosy retrospection-

The phenomenon of judging the past more positively than the present. Looking back at the past and thinking of it as much better even when it might not have been.


Echoic memory-

A momentary sensory memory of auditory stimuli.


Reconstruction memory-

The process of assembling info from stored memory when a clear memory doesn’t exist. An interviewer works with a crime victim to create a memory of the events surrounding a crime.


Intuition-

The product of instinct, feeling, and immediate insight.


Self-reference effect-

The tendency for people to encode things differently depending on if they are part of it or not. Things that are directly related to someone will be encoded better.


Nomenclature-

The choosing of names for things. Deciding to call a banana a banana.


Factor analysis-

A statistical procedure that identifies clusters of related items on a test.
