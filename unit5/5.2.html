Lesson 5.2 Encoding

We have mental representations in our brain of images that the cognitive system understands.


Encoding-

Some information, such as the route to home, is automatically processed. Novel information, like a new person’s name, requires attention and effort to process.


Automatic processing-

A lot of info is processed effortlessly like space, time, and frequency. We don’t have to rehearse this info to get it to our long-term memory. When reading a textbook, the place of a picture on the page is automatically encoded. When events happen in a day, they are unintentionally noted. We keep track unconsciously of how often things happen.


Effortful processing-

Novel info requires effort, like learning a concept from a text. Processing leads to durable and accessible memories; practice makes perfect is very true when it comes to memory.


Next in line effect-

Recall is better for what other people say but poor for a person just before you in line. You don’t pay attention to the person before, the info isn’t even getting to the brain, thus it is an encoding failure.


Spacing effect-

We retain info better when rehearsal is distributed over time. Studying a bit every night is better compared to cramming the night before a test.


Serial position (primacy recency) effect-

When recall is better for the first and last items, but poor for everything in between.


Visual encoding-

Mental imagery is a powerful aid to effortful processing, especially when combined with semantic encoding. Showing pictures of the side effects of smoking would be more powerful than just talking about it.


Acoustic encoding-

The encoding of sounds.


Active processing-

Actively visualizing the target to remember and putting it in memory.


Passive processing-

Just trying to memorize something without real processing for a short period.


Method of Loci (location)-

Involves moving through a familiar series of locations with items to be remembered.


Peg-word-

Memorizing a jingle and associating it with a list trying to remember.


Link method-

Forms a mental image of items to be remembered in a way that links them together.


Chunking-

Organizing items into familiar, manageable units, such as with acronyms.
