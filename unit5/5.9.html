Lesson 5.9 Intelligence Testing

Intelligence testing-

A method for assessing an individual’s mental aptitudes and comparing them with others using numerical scores.


Alfred Binet-

Binet and his colleague Theodore Simon developed the first standardized intelligence test while commissioned by the French government to create a test for children with problems in class.


Binet didn’t create the test to label children, he wanted to find a way to improve the educational experience of children.


Mental age-

The average age of people who achieve a level of performance. A 4-year-old child with the mental age of 6 would be academically advanced.


Lewis Terman-

In the US, he revised Binet’s test and named it the Stanford-Binet IQ test. Today, the Stanford-Binet is given orally and varies according to the tested. Children are given figures to copy or everyday problems to solve. Adults are asked to solve analogies, explain proverbs, and describe similarities in a set of words.


The exam starts by finding a mental age where all questions can be answered, moving to more difficult questions. The test ends when a mental age is reached where no questions can be answered.


William Stern-

Created the intelligence quotient, or IQ, formula.

IQ= (mental age/actual age) x100


David Wechsler-

Developed the Wechsler adult intelligence scale, or WAIS, and the Wechsler intelligence scale for children, or WISC, an intelligence test for preschoolers. These are the most widely used IQ tests today.


WAIS-

measures overall intelligence consisting of 2 parts, verbal and performance. Verbal includes vocabulary definitions and comprehension. Performance includes the timed assembly of small objects and arranged pictures in a logical order.


Achievement tests-

Reflect what you have already learned, your current level of knowledge.


Aptitude tests-

Predict your ability to learn new skills, how well you could do on something.


Principles of test construction-

For a test to be acceptable it must be standardized, reliable, and valid.


Standardization-

Test items piloted on a similar population and norms have been established. Norms are developed by calculating the average score achieved by a particular group and then each person’s score can be compared to see how it differs from people who took it before. Tests are periodically re-standardized and given to a random representative population sample to check the new norms.


Standardized tests establish a normal distribution of scores on a population, a bell-shaped pattern called the normal curve.


Flynn Effect-

The past 60 years have seen a rise in intelligence by 27 points.


Reliability-

A test that yields consistent results is reliable.


Split half reliability-

Dividing the test into two equal halves and assessing how consistent the scores are


Using different tests-

Using different tests to measure the consistency between them


Test-retest reliability-

Using the same test on two occasions to measure consistency.


Validity-

Reliability does not ensure validity; a test can be consistent without testing what it is supposed to test. Validity refers to what the test is supposed to measure or predict.


Content validity-

Refers to the extent a test measures a behavior/trait.


Predictive validity-

Refers to the function of a test in predicting a behavior/trait.


Fluid intelligence-

Reflects info-processing, reasoning, and memory skills. Declines as we age.


Crystallized intelligence-

Accumulated knowledge, skills, and experience. Increases with age.
