Lesson 5.3 Storage

The heart of memory is storage.


Long term memory-

Unlimited capacity to store information. It is estimated that the capacity ranges from 1,000-1,000,000 billion bits of information.


Not remembering pieces of information like who is on the front of a 20-dollar bill is an example of an encoding error.


Sorting memories-

Penfield concluded through electrical stimulation of the brain that old memories were etched into the brain.


Loftus reviewed this data and showed that only a handful of brain stimulated patients reported flashbacks.


Lashley using rats, suggested that even after removing arts of the brain animals retained part of the memories of the maze.


Memory trace-

Engram is the biological basis of long-term memory. It looks on the level of synapses and biochemical changes that are believed to represent the physical memory trace in nerve cells. It also looks for neural circuitry used by memory in the brain.


Synaptic changes-

Long term memories form at the synapse as fragile chemical traces that slowly consolidate into more permanent synaptic changes over time. The more we use synapses, the stronger the memory.


Neural circuitry-

Research began by looking at individuals that had parts of the brain removed in botched operations.


Patient HM had surgery for epileptic seizures, removing his hippocampus and amygdala on both sides of the brain. Since the surgery, he has been unable to create new memories although his memories of events prior to the operation remain normal. It shows that the neural circuitry we develop as we learn things stays even when parts of the brain disappear.


Brain structure involved in memory-

Hippocampus encodes and transfers new explicit memories to long term memory and aids in the initial encoding of information.


Cerebellum has memories involving movement.


Temporal lobe encodes and transfers new explicit memories to long term memory.


Amygdala encodes emotional aspects of memories. It strengthens memories that have strong emotional associations. These emotional connections aid access and retrieval. The amygdala is likely responsible for the memories associated with PTSD.


The prefrontal cortex has memories involving the sequences of events, but not the events themselves.


The cerebral cortex changes memories into more permanent ones.


Storage areas-

There isn’t any one place where memories are stored, they are spread out into neural networks all over the brain. It also depends on the material being learned. Storage appears to be linked to areas in the brain where the processing of the info occurs. Visual information memories would be stored in the visual cortex.


Heightened emotions create stronger memories. Stress seems to boost activity in the brain’s memory forming areas. Continued stress can disrupt memory.


Implicit memories-

Memories not consciously aware of but can affect behavior. Includes memories of skills like riding a bike and driving a car. You can dislike someone you met for the first time and dislike them because the implicit memory can remind you of someone bad.


Explicit memories-

Intentional or conscious recollection of info when trying to deliberately remember something. Remembering information during a test would be an explicit memory.


Procedural memories-

Memory for skills and habits. Most procedural memories are implicit because you don’t have to think about how to do it after learning. Walking, riding a bike, driving are examples.


Declarative memory-

Memory for factual info like names, faces, dates, etc. They are usually explicit memories that require conscious mental effort. Recalling the directions to drive somewhere is an example. They are declarative because you can declare you have the memory.


There are two types of declarative memory.


Semantic memory-

Memory for things with meaning like language, facts, general knowledge and concepts. This is a lot of the info learned in school.


Episodic memory-

Memory of biographical details of our lives. It stores temporal coding like time tags to indicate when and context coding to indicate where.
