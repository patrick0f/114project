Lesson 1.5 Experiments

Experiments-

Experiments are the only research method to isolate cause and effect. Experiments have hypotheses, a testable prediction.


Population-

A population is a group or category of people being researched.


A representative sample represents all people in a population.


Random sample is when everyone in the population has an equal chance of being chosen.


Stratified sample is when a population is divided into subcategories and a random sample is taken from each.


Random sampling happens so that the sample is representative of the population because researchers need to be able to generalize their findings to the whole population.


Random assignment is when the random sample is assigned to a experimental or control group. This minimizes preexisting differences between the groups so that it doesn’t impact the results of the experiment.


Experimental group is the group that receives the special treatment.


Control group is the comparison group that doesn’t receive the special treatment.


The independent variable (cause) is the factor being manipulated and studied.


The dependent variable (effect) is the factor that changes in response to the independent variable.


Both groups will receive the independent variable, the difference is in the degree of the variable they receive.


A placebo is a fake treatment. It tests the true effect of the independent variable since beliefs can alter behavior.


Placebo effect is when people don’t know they are getting a placebo and it still has the same effects regardless.


Controlling for bias-

Single blind research is when only the participants are unaware of the degree of the variable they receive.


Double blind research is when both participants and researchers are unaware of which group participants are in.


Confounding variables are outside variables that could affect the experiment other than the independent variable. Random assignment controls for these variables.
