Lesson 2.4 The Brain

The bottom of the brain, the brainstem, and the middle brain are the primitive early developed parts of the brain.


Techniques-

Brain lesions are when brain tissue is destroyed and studied to see what behaviors it has. Usually done for scientific or medicinal purposes.


Scans-

An electroencephalogram, EEG, is an amplified recording of the electrical waves sweeping across the brain's surface measured by electrodes on the scalp.


MRI scan (magnetic resonance imaging) uses magnetic fields and radio waves to make computer generated images that distinguish among types of soft brain tissue.


fMRI scan (functional MRI) measures brain activity by detecting changes associated with blood flow. The patient is shown information and the brain activity is reflected.


PET scan (positron emission tomography) observes metabolic processes in the body and brain through the ingestion of a kind of radioactive glucose that is measured by the machine.


CAT (computerized tomography/CT) scan combines x ray images taken from different angles of the brain to create cross sectional images of the brain and skull.


Parts of the brain-

Cerebral cortex is the top of the brain.


Corpus callosum connects/combines the two hemispheres.


Cerebellum is the mini brain in the back that controls movement, balance, and coordination.


Thalamus is in the center of the brain and receives info from the senses and sends out the info to the proper places.


Hypothalamus is under the thalamus and controls drives including hunger, thirst, sexual urges, and the pituitary gland.


Pituitary gland sends signals to your organs and glands using hormones to tell them what functions are needed and when


Pons controls the sleep stages and breathing


Medulla oblongata controls heartbeat


Reticular Activating system or reticular formation is a band of fibers that goes up the back of the brain stem. It controls alertness and reactions. Pons, medulla, and reticular formation are all part of the brain stem.


Spinal cord carries signals back and forth between your body and your brain


Amygdala controls extreme behaviors like fear and aggression


Hippocampus processes new memories


Limbic system-

A set of brain structures located on both sides of the thalamus that control emotion, behavior, motivation, learning, and memory. It includes the hippocampus, amygdala, and hypothalamus. Influences emotions during sleep.


Cingulate gyrus-

The fold covering the corpus callosum that processes emotions and also regulates behavior and automatic motor function.


Cerebral cortex vs Cerebrum-

Cerebral cortex is the outer layer of the cerebrum, the most prominent part of the brain. It is made of gray matter that covers the white matter of the cerebrum. It is responsible for sophisticated information processing in the brain.


Frontal lobe-

Also called the prefrontal cortex, it controls judgement, planning, speech sounds, emotions, personality, temperament, works with motor cortex movement, and censors the amygdala.


Case study: Phineas Gage-

A steel rod entered Gage’s eye and damaged his frontal lobe. He survived without any impairment to speech, motor, or memory. The lobe controlled planning and his temperament.


Somatoform/motor cortex-

Controls voluntary movement in peripheral nervous system, behind the frontal lobe. When parts of the motor cortex are stimulated, corresponding parts of the body will be moved. Movements that are precise are controlled by larger portions of the motor cortex.


Homunculus-

A homunculus is what the body would look like if it were proportionate to space taken up in the cortex. The hands would be enlarged since they require fine movements and a lot of space in the motor cortex.


Sensory/somatosensory cortex-

Behind the motor cortex, responsible for perceiving touch and pressure on the body. When stimulated, pressure on the corresponding part of the body would also be felt. The more sensitive the area, the more surface area on the cortex is taken up.


Parietal lobe-

Behind the somatosensory cortex, controls body position and spatial reasoning like touch, pressure, temperature, and pain.


Temporal lobe-

Near the ears, controls hearing with primary auditory cortex, stores long term memories, speech and language, and understanding


Occipital lobe-

Behind the parietal lobe, controls all vision with the primary visual cortex, and parts of the cortex corresponds with parts of the retina. It receives information from that place and the pieces are put together to make an image.


Association areas-

Uncommitted areas of the brain unrelated to sensory and motor functions that are used for higher mental functioning.


Wernicke’s Area-

In the left temporal lobe, responsible for language comprehension and expression.


Broca’s area-

In the left frontal lobe responsible for directing motor movement in speech.
