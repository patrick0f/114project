Lesson 8.6 Depressive and Bipolar Disorders

Depression-

The number one reason people seek mental health services. Women are twice as likely as men to experiences major depression, and ¼ of women will experience it in their lives. Increases with prevalence as new generations come.


Symptoms must last for two weeks without a cause, and 2 months for those grieving. They include depressed mood, loss of interest in activities, weight change, sleep issues, fatigue, feelings of worthlessness, and thoughts of suicide.


Being depressed is not being sad, it is depressed brain activity.


Biological perspective-

A lack of neurotransmitters: serotonin, norepinephrine, and dopamine can cause depression. Depression in the brain is simply a lack of certain neurotransmitters, especially serotonin.


Social cognitive perspective-

Negative thoughts influence negative behaviors and thought patterns, causing the depression cycle.


Depression cycle-

A negative, stressful event causes a pessimistic rumination (thinking) style which leads to a hopeless depressed state. This then hampers thinking and activity, fueling personal rejection and repeating the cycle.


Persistent depressive disorder-

Formerly dysthymia, a disorder marked by chronic low energy and poor self-esteem. It is seen as a lower grade longer lasting depression.


Bipolar disorder-

Formerly manic-depressive disorder, a disorder that alternates between depression and mania. Depressive symptoms include gloominess, withdrawnness, inability to make decisions, fatigue, and slowness of thought. Manic symptoms include elation, euphoria, desire to take action, hyper activeness, and a multitude of thoughts.


Bipolar I-

Those who experience manic episodes and major depressive episodes.


Bipolar II-

Those who experience a lesser mania, hypomania. Those with hypomania experience it for at least 4 days.


Cyclothymic disorder-

Those with hypomanic and depressive symptoms for at least 2 years but the symptoms don’t constitute a full major depressive episode or mania.


Biological causes-

Evidence for an inherited predisposition with bipolar disorder is strong. 2/3 bipolar people have a family history of mood disorders. Stressful events have also been found to trigger manic episodes.


PET scans show that brain activity rises and falls with manic and depressive episodes.
