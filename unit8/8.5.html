Lesson 8.5 Dissociative and Personality Disorders

Dissociation-

Getting lost in the moment while doing something. Characterized by separation from critical parts of the personality like memory, consciousness, or identity, that are normally integrated. Temporary dissociative states are common, like zoning out while driving.


Dissociative identity disorder-

Previously known as multiple personality disorder, it is a condition where a person seems to have several distinct personalities, each of which has a different personality. Different identities seem to have their own memories and desires, and often the impulses of one identity conflicts with that of others because there is only one body for the several personalities.


Shirley Mason Sybil-

A teacher with DID who suffered from blackouts and emotional breakdowns. She entered psychotherapy with Cornelia B Wilbur, a Freudian psychiatrist. She had 17 different personalities.


Psychoanalytic perspective-

A massive repression of unwanted impulses or memories creates a new person who acts out on these urges. The true identity can’t handle things, so a new personality is created.


Behaviorists-

Everyone is capable of acting differently in different situations, so DID is the extreme version of this acting.


Development of DID-

The disorder is so rare it is difficult to study its cause. Most clinicians believe that the root cause is abuse or trauma in childhood. Children separate themselves from the trauma to deal with the situation and continue this separation for their entire lives. There are some who don’t believe that DID is an actual disorder and that it is just people role playing to ease anxiety.


Personality disorders-

Long standing, inflexible personality traits that impair social functioning. Patients seek treatment at the insistence of others, as they aren’t distressed by their condition. There are three clusters of this disorder.


Cluster A-

Shows odd or eccentric behavior


Those who are paranoid have a pervasive, long-standing suspiciousness and generalized mistrust of others. Common beliefs include deception from others, untrustworthy peers, malicious use of information, hidden meaning in otherwise benign words, and unfaithful partners.


Schizoids have a pattern of indifference to social relationships with a limited range of emotional expression. This manifests itself in early adulthood through emotional detachment.


Schizotypal patients have a pattern of social and interpersonal difficulties which includes discomfort with close relationships, eccentric behavior, and unusual thoughts of reality. They have distorted thinking, strange behavior, and avoid intimacy.


Cluster B-

Shows dramatic or emotional traits


Borderline people are marked by a pattern of varying moods, self-image, and behavior, resulting in impulsive actions and conflicts in relationships.


Histrionic people are characterized by a pattern of excessive attention seeking emotions beginning in early childhood including seductive behavior and a need for approval.


Narcissistic people have a long-term pattern of abnormal behavior with exaggerated feelings of self-importance, an excessive need for admiration, and a lack of empathy.


Antisocial people are likely the most well-known disorder. They have a negative effect on society and a tendency to not obey the law, repeated deceitfulness, impulsivity, disregard for safety, and irritability. It can’t be diagnosed until 18 years of age, but to be diagnosed, individuals have to have shown symptoms since 15 years old. It occurs in males more often and sociopaths or psychopaths are associated with antisocials.


Causes may be an underdeveloped autonomic and central system, leading to low anxiousness and sensitivity to punishment, an underdeveloped amygdala, and bad environmental factors such as a lack of parental figures or poor discipline. PET scans show reduced activity in the frontal lobe. Forms of antisocial personality disorder appear in children, but they are considered neurodevelopmental disorders.


Conduct disorder is a repetitive pattern of behavior where the basic rights of others and norms are violated, referred to as antisocial behaviors.


Oppositional defiant disorder is a frequent tendency for anger, irritability, arguing, and defiance towards parents and authorities.


Cluster C-

Shows anxious or fearful behavior


Avoidant people have extreme social inhibition, inadequacy, and sensitivity to negative criticism, causing impairment to relationships.


Dependent people are unable to stay alone, showing anxiety when not around others and needing reassurance from others to function.


Obsessive compulsive personality disorder-

Characterized by a preoccupation with orderliness, perfection, and control at the expense of flexibility and efficiency.
