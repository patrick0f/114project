Lesson 8.2 Neurodevelopmental Disorders

Neurodevelopmental disorders-

A group of disorders that involve distortions in the development of basic psychological functions involved in social skills, language, perception, or motor behavior. They usually occur and are diagnosed in infancy, childhood, or adolescence.


Autism spectrum disorders-

A collection of disorders that show an unusual pattern of social and cognitive development starting in childhood that create difficulties in social interaction and communication. The spectrum includes high to low functioning individuals of the disorder.


The onset is usually before the age of 3, and research shows the earlier it is detected the more successful the treatment will be. It is more frequent in males, 4-5 times as likely to occur. 70% of those with autism disorder also have intellectual disabilities.


Characteristics-

Impairment of social relationships, lack of awareness of other’s feelings, not liking to be touched, avoiding eye contact, and preferring solitary play.


½ of autistic children don’t properly develop speech; speech is often described as an echo of what is being heard.


Many focus one object obsessively, having narrow interests in activities.


Cling to stability and routine, many become distraught if change occurs. Most engage in self soothing behaviors to block out extra sensory information, but do not seem to have a filter to block out irrelevant information nor do they have selective attention.


Causes-

Both genetics and the environment play a role. A number of genes have been identified to be associated with the disorder. Studies of those with autism have found irregularities in regions of the brain and abnormal levels of serotonin or other neurotransmitters.


Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)-

A developmental disability that involves a short attention span, high distractibility, and difficulty in remaining inactive for periods of time. There are 3 subtypes, predominantly hyperactive-impulsive, predominately inattentive, and combined hyperactive-impulsive and inattentive


Inattention-

Easily distracted, miss details, forgets things, and frequently switches from different activities. They also have difficulty learning new things, staying focused, and completing tasks properly. Don’t seem to listen when spoken to, daydream, become easily confused, have difficulty processing information, and struggle to follow instructions.


Hyperactivity-

Fidget when still, talk nonstop, always moving around and in motion, and has difficulty doing quiet tasks.


Impulsivity-

Very impatient, blurting out words, showing emotions without restraint, and interrupting others.


Causes-

A combination of genetic and environmental factors.


Treatment-

Stimulant drugs like Adderall, Ritalin, and Concerta improve the symptoms of the disorder and calming the patient. They do, however, tend to have side effects.


Related disabilities-

Learning disability, oppositional defiance disorder, conduct disorder, anxiety disorder, depression, bipolar disorder. One disorder doesn’t cause another but makes others more noticeable and severe.


Oppositional defiant disorder-

Behavior that starts around 8 but can be as early as preschool. Symptoms must last for at least 6 months and be more than just a normal childhood misbehavior. They include not following requests, being resentful of others, arguing with others, blaming others, having few friends, being in constant trouble, losing their temper often, being spiteful, and very easily annoyed.


Behavior is thought to be due to biopsychosocial factors.


Conduct disorder-

A disorder where children tend to be impulsive, hard to control, and unconcerned about others. More common among boys. It is associated with child abuse, family conflict, genetic defects, poverty, and parental drug addiction or alcoholism. Those with antisocial disorder were likely diagnosed with conduct disorder as a minor.


Symptoms include antisocial behaviors (behaviors that discourage social behavior) like bullying and fighting, rule breaking, cruel behavior towards others, destruction, heavy drinking/drug use, lying, running away, truancy, and vandalism.


Those with conduct disorder may develop personality disorders as adults, particularly antisocial personality disorder, and if it worsens, then it could also develop into significant drug and legal problems.
