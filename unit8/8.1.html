Lesson 8.1 Introduction to Psychological Disorders

1/4 of adults in the US experiences mental illness in a year. Many disorders like depression and schizophrenia exist in all cultures of the world.


American psychiatric association-

Includes the criteria for labelling behavior as disordered: a behavior or psychological syndrome that occurs in a person that reflects an underlying dysfunction, the consequences of which are distress, pain, disability, or impairment. It can’t be just a normal response to stressors or loss, but a result of social deviance or conflict.


Deviant-

Something that goes against the norm of behavior.


Distressful-

Something that causes distress or disturbance.


Dysfunctional-

Causes harm to self or others.


Unjustifiable-

Not reasonable.


History of disorders-

Ancient treatments of psychological disorders include trephination, exorcism, caging, beating, burning, castration, mutilation, and blood transfusion.


Trephination-

The boring of holes in the skull so as to remove evil forces.


Medical perspective-

Phillipe Pinel insisted that madness wasn’t due to demonic possession but due to mind ailments.


Medical model-

Medical health movement where mental illness was diagnosed on the basis of symptoms and treated through therapy, possibly in psychiatric hospitals.


Biopsychosocial approach-

Psychological disorders are created from a combination of factors.


Biological factors are evolution, individual genes, and brain structure/chemistry.


Psychological influences are stress, trauma, learned helplessness, and mood related perceptions/memories.


Socio-cultural influences are roles, expectations, and definitions of normality and disorder.


Psychoanalytic perspective-

Believes that psychological disorders come from unresolved childhood conflicts. To understand disorders, we must look at early life history.


Behavioral perspective-

Believes that behaviors are learned responses. To understand disorder, we must look at how behavior has been learned and what reinforces it.


Cognitive perspective-

Believes that thoughts and beliefs are the root of psychological problems. The only way to fix behavior is to change thoughts and beliefs.


Humanistic perspective-

Believes that people are responsible for their own abnormal behavior. Focuses on the relation between people and society and how people see themselves in relation to others. Believes that disordered people don’t have self-worth but have conditions of worth placed on them.


Socio-cultural perspective-

Believes abnormal behavior is shaped by family, society, and culture. One’s relationship with others can cause abnormal behaviors, and the stress in life can influence disorders.


Classifying psychological disorders-

The diagnostic and statistical manual of mental disorders is the source of classifying psychological disorders, made by the American psychiatric association. It describes 400 disorders in the newest fifth edition.


The book has three sections, the introduction, a list of disorders, and measures for diagnosis. The DSM aims to describe disorders and indicate how prevalent they are. The disorders are reliable, thus diagnosis by professionals is consistent.


The DSM is criticized for categorizing any kind of behavior in the scope of psychiatry. Labels can stigmatize people by assigning them arbitrary conditions and judging their value. They can, however, assist in medical communication and therapy establishment.


Rosenhan’s experiment on being sane in insane places-

The researcher feigned a mental disorder and admitted himself into a psychiatric hospital. They claimed they heard the word thud as a symptom. This experiment showed issues about diagnosis in the treatment.


Insanity-

Raises ethical questions about how we should treat those with disorders and criminals.
