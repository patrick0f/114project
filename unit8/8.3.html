Lesson 8.3 Schizophrenia

Psychosis-

A symptom in which thoughts and emotions are so impaired that contact is lost with external reality.


Schizophrenia spectrum disorder-

On a spectrum that encompasses the varying degrees of symptoms a schizophrenic person can have.


1/100 people suffer from it, and it appears in maturing young people around the age of 18-20. Both men and women are affected, but men are hit by it harder.


Those with schizophrenia often have fragmented, bizarre thinking with false beliefs called delusions.


Disorganized thinking-

Believed to occur because of selective attention failure that results in fragmented, bizarre thoughts spilling out in random order.


Disorganized perceptions-

A schizophrenic person can perceive things that aren’t there, hallucinations.


Inappropriate emotions and actions-

Emotions or actions that aren’t appropriate to the situation. A schizophrenic person can laugh at the news of death or be completely apathetic, referred to as flat affect.


Positive symptoms-

The adding of normal function, the presence of inappropriate behavior. Distortion or excess of normal functions that are most frequent in the first stages of schizophrenia. Includes delusions, hallucinations, disordered speech, and disorganized behavior.


Delusions-

Distorted false beliefs. Thinking you are a mighty superhero instead of a normal office worker.


Hallucinations-

Perception without the corresponding sensation. Includes mostly auditory hallucinations, but sometimes can be visual, olfactory, or gustatory.


Disorganized speech-

Disordered thoughts that lead to jumbled words with no actual sense to them.


Disorganized behavior-

Inappropriate motor behaviors that are senseless and compulsive, like continuously rocking in a chair.


Negative symptoms-

The removing of normal functions, the absence of appropriate behavior. Behavioral deficits that decrease normal function. Includes flat affect, avoiding eye contact, expressionless face, apathy, poverty of speech, long and slowed speech, slowed movements, and lack of directedness.


Chronic process schizophrenia-

Slow developing schizophrenia with a harder recovery process. This kind displays negative symptoms.


Acute reactive schizophrenia-

Rapidly developing schizophrenia with a better recovery process. This kind displays positive emotions.


Paranoia-

A common characteristic in schizophrenics where people have a preoccupation with delusions and hallucinations, with themes of persecution or grandiosity.


Catatonic-

Immobility or purposeless movements with extreme negativism, and/or mimicking of another’s speech and movements.


Childhood schizophrenia-

A type of schizophrenia where a person loses touch with reality, psychosis. Occurs early in life and impacts children’s behavior. Includes hallucinations, delusions, irrational behavior, and problems carrying out daily tasks.


Brain abnormalities-

Dopamine overactivity and higher levels of dopamine D4 receptors in the brain have been shown in schizophrenics. Brain scans show abnormal activity in the frontal cortex, thalamus, and amygdala. Adolescent schizophrenic patients have been seen to have brain lesions.


Schizophrenics also show low activity in the frontal lobes. High activity in the thalamus causes hallucinations. The more the hippocampus and amygdala is shrunken, the worse the disorder is. If the thalamus is small, filtering information and reactions will worsen.


Causes-

Prenatal problems such as low birth weight, birth complications, and fetal infections such as influenza can cause these brain abnormalities. Someone with an identical twin that has schizophrenia is 50% more likely to also have it themselves.


Early signs of schizophrenia include maternal schizophrenia, birth complications, short attention span, poor muscle coordination, disruptive/withdrawn behavior, emotional unpredictability, and poor peer relations.
