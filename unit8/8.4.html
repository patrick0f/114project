Lesson 8.4 Anxiety, OCD, and Trauma Related Disorders

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental illness in the US. Anxiety used to be a large all-encompassing umbrella for disorders in the DSMIV but is now counted separately in the DSMV from obsessive compulsive disorders and trauma/stress related PTSD disorders.


Anxiety disorders-

General qualities include worry, fear, apprehension, intrusive thoughts, physical symptoms, and feelings of tension. These have an automatic quality that comes from within, like an uncontrollable reaction.


General anxiety disorder-

Prolonged, vague, but intense fears that aren’t attached to any particular object. 4-6% of the population experiences GAD, and it is more common in women and starts showing in young adults.


Symptoms include excessive anxiety for at least 6 months, inability to control worry, considerable impairment in important parts of life, and other symptoms such as restlessness, fatigue, loose concentration, irritability, and tension.


Panic disorder-

Indicators are similar to GAD, but more magnified and spontaneous. Disorder is more common among women and young people. Clinical features include recurring, unexpected panic attacks that last for a month and a constant worry of having them again. Symptoms include shortness of breath, dizziness, trembling, sweating, racing heart, choking, nausea, numbness, chest pain, and a sense of going crazy.


Phobia-

Marked by a persistent and irrational fear of an object or situation that disrupts behavior. Common fears include animals, heights, blood, and close spaces. People with phobias know what they are afraid of, but their fear is unproportionate with reality.


Specific phobia-

Fear of an object, such as spiders or closed spaces.


Social phobia-

Fear of embarrassment in interaction with others.


Agoraphobia-

Fear of fearful events and fear itself, often accompanied by panic attacks. People with this phobia might not even leave their home in fear.


Obsessive compulsive disorder-

The persistence of unwanted intrusive thoughts, obsessions, and the urge to engage in senseless rituals, compulsions, that cause distress. These compulsions act to alleviate the obsessions. A person has an obsession or intrusive thought, followed by anxiety or stress, and thus carries out a ritual, which brings relief. This repeats in a cycle. Those with OCD don’t trust their own sense, so they repeat their compulsions over and over. A biological psychologist would say that the frontal lobe is affected by OCD. PET scans of OCD patients show high metabolic activity in the frontal lobe areas that are involved with attention.


Body dysmorphic disorder-

An OCD related disorder that used to be considered a somatoform disorder because it was of the body, along with trichotillomania and excoriation disorder. A preoccupation or belief in an imagined or exaggerated defect in personal appearance. It is often about the shape of facial features, skin blemishes, or thinning hair. BDD patients experience great stress or self-consciousness over this deformity. It becomes evident in adolescence and affects both genders equally.


Hoarding disorder-

An OCD related disorder where there is a difficulty with discarding possessions because of a need to save them and a sense of distress at the thought of getting rid of items.


Trichotillomania-

An OCD related disorder where there is a hair pulling disorder that involves recurrent, irresistible urges to pull out hair from the body.


Excoriation disorder-

An OCD related disorder with a skin picking disorder where one repeatedly picks at the skin, resulting in lesions and disruption to life.


Post-traumatic stress disorder-

Four or more weeks of experiences haunting memories, nightmares, flashbacks, social withdrawal, jump anxiety, and sleep problems can constitute PTSD.


A common prototype for PTSD is a soldier, but anyone who experiences trauma can get PTSD.


10% of women and 20% of men who experience trauma develop PTSD. Holocaust survivors have shown resilience against traumatic situations, but PTSD is still common among them. PTSD can also be caused by non-combat related trauma such as sexual or physical abuse, car accidents, natural disasters, and even medical procedures.


Acute stress disorder-

Very similar to PTSD but starts immediately after the trauma and lasts up to a month. PTSD and acute stress disorder were previously considered anxiety disorders, but not anymore due to patients not always experiencing anxiety.


Learning perspective-

Fear conditioning leads to anxiety, which gets associated with other objects due to stimulus generalization and reinforced thereafter.


Biological perspective-

Anxiety, panic attacks, and OCD are linked with brain circuits like the anterior cingulate cortex.
