Lesson 7.6 Sexual Needs

Biopsychosocial influences of sex-

Biological influences include sexual maturity, sex hormones (testosterone and estrogen), and sexual orientation.


Psychological influences include exposure to stimulating conditions and sexual fantasies.


Socio-cultural influences include familial and societal values, religious and personal values, and cultural expectations.


Study of sex-

The first major study of sex was in the 1940s with researcher Alfred Kinsey who interviewed 17,000 people concerning their sexual behavior. He rated them from 0-6 on a scale of exclusively heterosexual to exclusively homosexual.


Sexual response cycle-

Masters and Johnson brought sex to a laboratory and described human sexual response as consisting of 4 phases in sequential order.


The first phase is excitement, where the genitals become engorged with blood; the vagina expands and secretes lubricant while the penis enlarges.


The second phase is plateauing, where excitement such as breathing, pulse, and blood pressure peaks.


The third phase is orgasm where contractions occur all over the body, there is an increase in breathing, pulse, and blood pressure, and sexual release occurs.


The fourth phase is resolution where the engorged genitals release blood. Males will go into their refractory phase while women resolve themselves slower.


Sexual scripts-

Socially learned programs of sexual interpretation and responsiveness.


Sexual orientation-

A person’s preference for emotional and sexual relationships with individuals of the same sex, other sex, and/or either sex.


Homosexuals have a preference of those same sex, heterosexuals have a preference of those opposite sex, and bisexuals don’t have a preference for either; they like both.


Psychologists see sexual orientation as neither willingly chosen nor changed. The view of homosexuality remains controversial today due to societal, political, and moral factors. Many scientific theories point to genetics for causes of sexual orientation.


Biological sexual orientation-

The size of the anterior hypothalamus is smaller, and the anterior commissure is larger in homosexual men, suggesting there are biological differences in homosexual men.


Homosexuality seems to run in families and is more common in twins (more so identical than fraternal).


Genetic engineers can genetically manipulate female flies to act like males during courtship and males to act like females.


Fetal developments-

Prenatal hormones affect sexual orientation during fetal development.


In female sheep, testosterone exposure in the fetus led to increased homosexual behavior.


In humans, female hormone exposure (estrogen) to fetuses resulted in attraction to males, regardless of the fetus’ gender.
