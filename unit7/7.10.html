Lesson 7.10 Humanistic Theories of Personality

By the 60s psychologists became unhappy with Freud’s negative mindset and the mechanistic psychology of behaviorists.


Humanistic theory-

Emphasizes that individuals control their own behavior. Views human nature in a positive light and believes in innate goodness. Popular in the 60s because of its person-centered ideas.


Self-actualization-

The process of working to become the best person you can be.


Self-actualizing person-

Maslow believed that we try to reach the state of self-actualization or fulfilling our own unique potential. This desire exists in all but is often blocked by the environment.


Self-actualizing characteristics include accepting the self unconditionally, being spontaneous and natural, having a democratic nature, liking privacy, focusing on external problems, having a strong ethical and moral sense, having a small and close friend group, and being realistic.


Carl Rogers-

Believed that people are naturally good. He thought that the central feature of our personality is our self-concept. He thought that people must have 3 core concepts in order to truly grow.


Genuineness is the ability to be open with one’s feelings and drop facades of emotion.


Acceptance is getting rid of conditions of worth, thinking that you must accomplish something in order to be truly worthy, and offering unconditional positive regard.


Empathy is sharing our feelings and reflecting meanings, putting yourself in other’s shoes.


Rogers asked people to describe themselves as they would like to be, their ideal selves, and as they actually are, their real selves. If the descriptions were close, then they had a positive self-concept.


Self-concept-

Our perception of our abilities, behaviors, and characteristics. If it is positive, then we act in positive ways.


Humanistic perspective-

Has a great impact on counseling, education, child rearing, and management. The concepts can be vague and subjective while lacking scientific basis. Individualism can lead to selfishness and corruption.
