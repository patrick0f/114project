Lesson 7.2 Experiencing Emotion

Unconscious expression of emotions-

We have a fast response system that relies on deep brain circuitry to operate automatically and is linked to implicit memories. It acts as a defense that produces emotions to help us get out of dangerous situations. We are receptive to possible threats because of the amygdala, which looks for potential subliminal threats. This system is quick to learn about emotional responses through classical conditioning but is slow to forget.


Conscious expression of emotions-

Generates emotions slower, but delivers more complete information to the consciousness, linked with explicit memories. Relies heavily on the cerebral cortex.


Limbic system and amygdala-

Processes extreme emotions like fear and aggression and is always alert for threats, contributing to fight or flight response.


Reticular formation-

Works with thalamus and amygdala to monitor incoming info. If a threat is detected, it sets responses in motion that arouse the body. The responses in the brain cause heart, respiration, and muscle tension.


Cerebral cortex-

Interprets events and associates them with memories and feelings. The right side has negative emotions, and the left side has positive emotions.


Autonomic nervous system-

The Sympathetic nervous system becomes active when startled (fight or flight) or experiencing an unpleasant emotion.


The parasympathetic nervous system dominates pleasant emotions.


Hormones-

Serotonin is involved in feelings of depression, epinephrine is produced in fear, and norepinephrine is more abundant in anger.


Basic emotions-

Izard said we have 10 basic emotions: joy, excitement, surprise, sadness, anger, disgust, contempt, fear, shame, and guilt.


Fear-

Learned through conditioning and observation. We are biologically predisposed to fear things because the response helps us survive.


The amygdala plays a key role in associating emotions with situations. If the amygdala is damaged, the fear response could be diminished due to a lack of association.


Conditioning-

Associating emotions with events


Observation-

Watching others display emotions in response to stimuli


Anger-

Chronic hostility is linked to heart disease. Controlled expression of anger is good, better than pent up or irrational anger.


Catharsis hypothesis-

Releasing aggressive energy relieves aggressive urges. Research has shown that expressing anger can be temporarily calming, but it doesnâ€™t clear feelings of rage. The expression of anger can lead to more hostility due to the reinforcement experienced if not careful.


Happiness-

Those who are happy see the world as safe, make decisions easier, are more cooperative, and live happier. Significant negative events seldom destroy happiness permanently.


Feel-good do-good phenomenon-

People are helpful to others when they are in a good mood.


Adaption level phenomenon-

Our tendency to judge stimuli as relative to those previously experienced. We have neutral levels and react to variations as they occur up and down from these levels. Winning the lottery would lead to a surge of initial happiness, but then adaptation would occur, and it would become the new norm.
