Lesson 7.9 Psychoanalytic Theories of Personality

Neo-Freudians-

From psychoanalytic to psychodynamic, Alfred Adler believed in childhood tensions that were social in nature, not sexual.


Inferiority complex-

A child’s struggle during growth for superiority and power.


Karen Horney-

Believed in the social aspect of childhood growth and that children are trying to overcome helplessness.


She countered Freud’s assumption that women have weak superegos and suffer from penis envy.


Penis envy-

Freud’s assumption that since women don’t have penises and men do, and men have power in society, that women are envious of men’s penises due to their association with power.


Collective consciousness-

A common reservoir of images derived from our species’ past that is full of archetypes, believed in by Carl Jung.


Archetype-

Universal, symbolic images that appear across cultures through stories and art.


Projective test-

A psychological instrument that reveals the hidden unconscious mind. A psychoanalyst projects their interpretations of the results of the tests to derive meaning. The two main tests are the thematic apperception test and the Rorschach inkblot test.


Critics say that projective tests lack reliability in the consistency of the results and validity in the prediction of the results. Train raters evaluating the same patient can have different unreliable interpretations, and these tests can misdiagnose a normal person as invalidly pathological.


Thematic apperception test-

Developed by Henry Murray, a projective test where people express their feelings and interests through the stories they make up about ambiguous scenes.


Rorschach inkblot test-

The most widely used projective test that has 10 inkblots created by Herman Rorschach. These blots serve to identify people’s inner feelings by analyzing their interpretations.
