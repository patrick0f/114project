Lesson 7.12 Trait Theory

Trait-

A tendency to respond in a certain way in different kinds of situations. Trait theorists believe that every trait applies to all people and the trait can be measured. Theorists look for connections between a person’s behaviors.


Gordon Allport-

Described personality in terms of fundamental traits, or characteristic patterns of behavior to feel or act a certain way. He identified the cardinal trait, central trait, and secondary trait.


Cardinal trait-

A characteristic or feature so critical that it defines a person. Martin Luther King was a righteous and passionate man.


Central trait-

Traits that make people predictable. A shy boy doesn’t like talking to people.


Secondary trait-

Traits that convey preferences such as for music or food. Someone likes pop music or Italian food.


Raymond Cattell-

Used statistics and factor analysis to identify 16 personality factors that he believed were the building blocks of personality. Everyone has these 16 factors, just to different degrees.


Eysenck and Eysenck-

Said that there were 2 basic dimensions of personality that were rated on a continuum. Extraversion means being sociable, outgoing, and lively, while introversion means being thoughtful, reserved, and quiet.


Emotional stability means being relaxed, adjusted, and even-tempered, while emotional instability means being moody, anxious, and restless.


Big five-

A set of expanded factors for an approximation of basic trait dimensions. A compilation of work from many researchers and the most accepted trait theory in psychology. These traits are rated on a continuum.


Emotional stability here is individuals who experience things easily without getting upset. The opposite is emotional instability or neuroticism, which is those who are always angry and look at the bad rather than the good.


Extroversion is associated with being energetic, and the opposite is introversion, being quiet and shy.


Agreeableness is being sympathetic, cooperative, and kind, and the opposite is antagonism, being irritable, suspicious, and jealous.


Openness describes those who are open-minded and willing to try new ideas, and the opposite is resistant to new experiences, being reserved and unspontaneous.


Conscientiousness is being dutiful, dedicated, and organized, and the opposite is impulsiveness, being careless, irresponsible, and quitting easily.


Personality inventory-

Questionnaires used to assess traits that ask people to respond to items covering feelings and behavior.


The most used inventory is the MMPI-2, or Minnesota multiphasic personality inventory, that was developed to identify abnormal personality tendencies. It scores objectively, meaning there is a formula for the answers.


Personality change-

Traits appear to remain constant over time and situations, but behaviors can change. People don’t act in a predictable manner all the time, but one’s average personality is predictable. The social situation has a large impact on our behavior.


Other Important Vocabulary

Psychophysiological illness-

Any stress related physical illness, like a headache.


Well-being-

Self-perceived happiness or satisfaction with life


Lymphocytes-

The two types of white blood cells that are part of the immune system. Their production is impaired when there is excess stress.


Health psychology-

A subfield of psychology that provides contribution to behavioral medicine.


Psychoneuroimmunology-

The study of how psychological, neural, and endocrine processes affect the immune system and its health. Includes stress and its effect on the immune system.


Coronary heart disease-

The clogging of the heart vessels.


Glucose-

A form of sugar that circulates in the blood and provides energy for the body.


Relative deprivation-

The perception that we are worse off compared to those we compare ourselves with.


Behavioral medicine-

A field that integrates behavioral and medical knowledge to apply it to health and disease.


Ostracism-

An extreme form of rejection where one is excluded in the presence of others. Show to increase aggression.


Carbohydrates-

Consumption of carbs will trigger the release of serotonin, reducing tension and anxiety.


Neophobia-

The dislike of unfamiliar new things


Instinct theory-

Instincts provide humans with certain predispositions and drive them to do certain things.


Spillover effect-

When attitudes in one role positively spill over into another. Being aroused by intense exercise and then having a strong reaction to finding 5 dollars missing.


Anterior cingulate cortex-

Part of the brain that increases activity when exposed to pain and social exclusion.


Macrophage-

Immune cells that seek and destroy unwanted material.


Tend and befriend syndrome-

A behavior exhibited in response to threat where species will seek out their social group for protection and protect their offspring.


FTO gene-

A gene associated with a predisposition to obesity.


Fraternal birth order effect-

The tendency for older brothers to increase the odds of homosexuality in later males.


Leptin-

Hunger dampening protein secreted by fat cells.


Ghrelin-

The hunger hormone secreted by the stomach


Orexin-

A hunger regulating neuropeptide secreted by the lateral hypothalamus.


Terror management theory-

A theory of death related anxiety that explores peoples emotional and behavioral responses to reminder of their death.


Spotlight effect-

The tendency to overestimate others’ notice of one’s appearance, performance, and mistakes.


Positive psychology-

The study of optimal human functioning that aims to promote strengths and virtues of humans.


Empirically derived test-

A test developed by selecting the ones that discriminate between groups.


Possible selves-

How an individual thinks about their potential future. Someone could imagine themselves as rich and successful in the future or poor and destitute due to poor choices.
