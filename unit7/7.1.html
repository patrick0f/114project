Lesson 7.1 Theories of Emotion

Emotions are a mix of physiological activation, expressive behaviors, and conscious experience.


James Lange Theory-

William James and Carl Lange proposed an idea that was opposed to common sense view. It proposed that physiological activity preceded emotional experience; bodily changes cause the feeling of emotions. Stimulus activates physiological arousal, which causes emotion.


Seeing a strange man outside causes the sympathetic nervous system to fire up and the heartbeat to raise. This creates the emotion of fear.


Cannon Bard Theory-

Walter Cannon and Philip Bard questioned the James Lange theory and proposed that emotion triggering stimuli and body arousal take place simultaneously. The thalamus produces both physical and emotional reactions simultaneously as it sends messages through the body. Stimulus activates both physiological arousal and emotion.


Someone suddenly touching you would cause you to tense up and become startled simultaneously.


Two factor theory-

Stanley Schachter and Jerome Singer suggested that psychiatry and cognition creates emotions. These emotions have two factors, physical arousal and cognitive label. We think about environmental cues and the kind of physical arousal to cognitively label the emotion.


A stranger starts waving at you, which leads to an increased heartbeat and breathing rate (physiology), but they are smiling (cognitive label), so you end up with a happy emotion instead of fear because you recognize that he is friendly.


Physiological similarities-

Physiological responses are similar across emotions. Excitement and fear involve similar physiological arousal. Being scared would lead to heightened tension, heartbeat, sweating, and shaking. In a state of anger, similar physical responses would occur.


Physical responses like finger temperature and facial muscles change during fear, rage, and joy.


The amygdala shows differences in activation during emotions. The left hemisphere, happy emotions, is different from the right hemisphere, depressed emotions.


Two routes to emotion-

Zajonc and LeDoux say that some emotions are immediately processed without conscious appraisal. Our emotional reactions can be quicker than our interpretation of events; we feel before we think. Some neural pathways involved in emotion bypass cortical areas involved in thinking and go directly to the amygdala.


Lazarus, Schachter, and Singer emphasize that appraisal determines emotions.
