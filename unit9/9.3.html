Lesson 9.3 Negative Social Influences

Prejudice-

Also called prejudgment, an unjustifiable, negative attitude towards a group, often of different backgrounds. Composed of beliefs, stereotypes, emotions, and predispositions to act.


Works at the conscious and more unconscious levels, like a knee jerk response rather than a decision.


Causes include social inequality, division, identity, thought patterns, and emotional scapegoating.


Ingroup-

People that share a common identity with someone. Can be family, friends, ethnic group, etc.


Outgroup-

Those seen as different from the ingroup.


Ingroup bias-

The tendency to favor one’s own group/ingroup.


Jane Elliot-

Discriminated against brown and blue-eyed students. She identified the brown eyed students as inferior, causing friends to turn on each other. She did the same for the blue-eyed students.


Scapegoat theory-

When prejudice is an outlet for anger to blame someone. Nazis blamed Jews for the years of economic despair as a scapegoat. Arabs were lashed out against after 9/11.


Cognitive roots-

The brain uses shortcuts for everything, so it categorizes people into easily identifiable traits, like looks. We stereotype those groups based on previous experiences.


Just world phenomenon-

Good things happen to good people and bad things happen to bad people. People get what they deserve, which is victim blaming.


Contact hypothesis-

Stereotypes and prejudice against a group will diminish as contact with them increases. This is only part of the solution; educational techniques must also be used to address social cognitions and perceptions.


Aggression-

A range of behaviors that result in physical and psychological harm to others/oneself.


Genetic influences-

Animals have been bred for aggressiveness. Twin studies show that aggression may be genetic and is possibly linked to the male Y chromosome.


Neural influences-

The limbic system, amygdala, and frontal lobe are involved in aggression.


Biochemical influences-

Animals that see decreased testosterone through castration become docile. Testosterone injections also increase aggression levels. Prenatal testosterone exposure also increases aggression in female hyenas.


Cultural influences-

When aggression leads to a desired outcome, one learns to be aggressive, which is seen more in individualist cultures.


Frustration aggression principle-

Frustration creates anger, which leads to aggression. This means people won’t be aggressive unless they are frustrated. Researchers agree that the direct cause of most aggression is a negative emotion.


Excitation transfer-

Arousal from an experience can carry over to another situation. This new situation must trigger aggression. After running a mile, you discover your car got pooped on, so you respond aggressively due to your already heightened state.


Aversive events and people-

Those that are miserable often make others miserable.


Stressful environments-

High temperatures, air pollution, and noise have been shown to increase aggressive tendencies.
