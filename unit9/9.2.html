Lesson 9.2 Social Influences

Social influence-

How the actions of others shape the way we think/behave.


Social roles-

Socially defined patterns of behavior expected of people in a setting. Prescribes behavior by saying what to do, how to do it, and when. The roles people assume can result from their interests or goals, but also by cultural economic, or biological conditions.


Social norms-

A group’s expectations about what is appropriate and acceptable for its member’s attitudes and behaviors. It can be broad, like certain political views or narrow like having to raise your hand to speak. How you greet others is a different social norm in different cultures.


Conformity-

Adjusting one’s behavior to coincide with the group standard.


We follow other’s behaviors to conform. The spectrum goes from conformity to compliance to obedience, from least to most extreme guidance.


Conditions that influence conformity include the size of the group, status of the group, observation of the group, and unanimity of the group.


Solomon Asch-

Conducted a line experiment to test levels of conformity. Individual participants would sit with other pseudo participants who would give the wrong answer collectively to see if the actual participant would conform or not. 1/3 of participants conformed to the unanimous group regularly, and ¾ conformed at least once.


Normative social influence-

You want to avoid rejection or gain approval, so you respect the norms.


Informative social influence-

The group may provide valuable info, so you try to accept their opinions, especially in tough decisions.


Compliance-

Adjusting behavior because of a request. Explicit is a direct request, implicit is an indirect request. Implicit compliance is often used by politicians or salesmen.


Obedience-

A change in behavior in response to the command of an authority.


Milgram’s shock experiment-

Stanley Milgram designed a study that investigated how authority affects obedience. An experimenter, an actor, and a participant went in a room where the actor would get hooked into a shock machine. It tested how far the participant would go to shock someone because an authority figure (experimenter) told them to. 65% of participants went all the way to shock actors for 450 volts.


People rationalized their obedience because they felt the experimenter was responsible for what happened to the other person. The prestige of an authority figure makes it harder to disobey. The presence of others that disobey brings down obedience. Personality of the person also changes obedience.


Social dilemmas-

Situations where selfish behavior that benefits people in the short term will cause disaster for the group in the long term. A person can either rat out his friend and get off free or keep quiet and both receive a smaller punishment.


Social facilitation-

The improved performance of task in the presence of others. Playing a sport better in the presence of a friend.


Social loafing-

The tendency for people in a group to be less effortful compared to when they are an individual. They feel less accountable in a group and see contribution as less important.


Deindividuation-

Mob mentality or the loss of self-awareness and restraint in a group situation that has anonymity and a fervorous atmosphere. Nobody can be singled out, so people feel generally less accountable.


Group polarization-

Enhances prevailing attitudes in group discussion. A like-minded group will strengthen the prevailing opinions. It can be beneficial or harmful, but most generally leave energized and passionate.


Group thinking-

A way of thinking that occurs when the desire for harmony in decision making overrides the alternatives. The general consensus prevails, and the minority’s voice is blocked out.
