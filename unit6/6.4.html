Lesson 6.4 Adolescence

Adolescence begins with puberty, around age 11-14, when sexual organs mature and ends at the beginning of independent adulthood. Self-image can be greatly impacted when one physically matures.


Early maturing boys tend to be more athletic, popular, and have a more positive self-concept. However, they have more difficulty in school, commit more acts of delinquency, and may become involved with substance abuse. In the long run, early maturing boys are more responsible and cooperative in later life.


Boys that develop later are smaller and less coordinated, looking less attractive.


Early maturing girls are more sought after romantically and have more self-esteem than later maturing girls. Early development can be embarrassing for girls.


Girls that develop later have higher satisfaction with their bodies in later years because they tend to be tall and slim, the societal standard for beauty.


Cognitive development-

By adolescence, we are in the formal operational stage, and begin to reason hypothetically and deduce consequences, leading to greater understanding of moral principles.


Lawrence Kohlberg-

Believed that moral reasoning helped guide judgement and behavior and proposed dilemmas for reasoning at different levels. His theory of moral development assumes that people go through levels in a fixed order. There are no real ages associated with the levels, but he believed that that the highest level required at least 13 years old. His theory had 3 levels.


Preconventional-

Young children perform actions based on reward/punishment and are selfish or self-centered. If someone receives a reward for doing something, they would be in the preconventional stage.


Conventional-

Being interested in pleasing others and doing what is right socially. Considering more than the self, thinking of family and society as a whole.


Postconventional-

Uses higher reasoning to guide behavior, thinks about problems in terms of justice and basic human rights. Kohlberg didn’t think that many people made it to this level of reasoning.


Erik Erikson-

Created the idea of psychosocial development, which involves interacting with one another and knowledge of oneself as a member of society. He believed that passage through the stages requires the resolution of a conflict.


Trust vs Mistrust-

The first stage is from birth to 18 months old. Babies are dependent on others to meet their physical and emotional needs.


If parents care for the baby and create a sense of attachment, then it will develop trust and predictability. If the care is inconsistent or lacking, then the baby will develop mistrust and paranoia.


Autonomy vs Shame and doubt-

The second stage is from 18 months to 3 years old. If parents set boundaries but let their child make decisions, then they will develop a will or sense of independence. If the child is overly restricted, then children will be unable to assert themselves. If the parents are too lenient, the child will become overly demanding and controlling, creating a sense of shame and self-consciousness.


Initiative vs guilt-

The third stage is from 3-6 years old. Children start developing their imaginations, curiosity, and independence.


If parents react positively, their child will feel confident to take risks and have self-confidence. If parents react negatively, their child will lack self-sufficiency, self-esteem, and will fear punishment.


Industry vs inferiority-

The fourth stage is from 6-11 years old. Children start to compare themselves in social and academic aspects.


Those that feel they can do tasks will have a sense of pride and accomplishment. Those that can’t will feel inferior to others that can.


Identity vs role confusion-

The fifth stage is from 12-20 years old. Teens start transitioning to adulthood and try to discover who they are and what roles they are fit for. Pressures to figure out what one wants to do with their life are felt. Teens will start separating themselves from their parents and relying on their peers. Here, Erikson believed that one had to find themselves and figure out their role before being able to truly express love.


Some have a sense for who they are and have a commitment to their future. Others have confusion over what their role is, which can lead to an unstable identity and the adoption of a socially unacceptable role.


Intimacy vs isolation-

The sixth stage from age 20-30. People begin pondering entering a close and loving relationship.


They either develop intimacy and a sense of connectedness or develop isolation where they draw themselves in and avoid emotional contact.


Generativity vs Stagnation-

The seventh stage from age 30-65. People ponder the sense of making a difference through their children, occupation, and community involvement.


Those that are happy with their life and feel that they have made a difference will have a sense of accomplishment. Those that don’t will feel worthless and potentially go through a mid-life crisis.


Integrity vs despair-

The last stage from age 65 onwards. People will look back on their life and their actions.


Those who see their life happily will have a strong sense of peace and acceptance. Those who see otherwise will develop a sense of despair and look back with a sense of frustration, regret, and disappointment.
