Lesson 6.5 Adulthood Develpment

Early and middle adulthood-

At the range of 20-65. Early adulthood is centered around careers. The midlife transition occurs in a person’s 40s and is where one may question their life and accomplishments, mainly due to the idea of death. For most, the process of middle age is calm, focusing on the present and coming to terms with their decisions.


People are still getting married, but rates of living together and divorce are higher than before. Around 90% of heterosexual adults marry and 75% of divorcees will remarry. 50% of first marriages end in divorce.


Women’s roles-

Close to 75% of married women with children work outside the home. Most of those working will come home to do housework. Employed mothers put in an extra month of 24 hours days during the course of a year, yet most working women still report personal satisfaction and a sense of contribution.


Late adulthood-

The age of 65 and onwards. Many physical changes in late adulthood include thinning gray hair, wrinkly skin, and a loss of height. The senses are dulled, reaction times are slowed, and stamina is depleted.


Genetic programming theory of aging-

A kind of built-in time limit for the reproduction of cells exist, and the self-destruct button gets pushed at this time.


Wear and tear theory of aging-

The body’s mechanical functions stop working as efficiently as people age.


Cognitive changes-

Skills related to fluid intelligence decline in old age, but skills related to crystallized intelligence remain strong. Neural processing slows down which makes it so memory processing takes longer. Memory loss in old age is limited to episodic memories. If a person stays physically and mentally active, memory loss will be almost nonexistent. However, diseases like Alzheimer’s can cause memory loss.


Disengagement theory of aging-

Aging produces a gradual withdrawal from the world on a physical, psychological, and social level. It provides an opportunity for reflection and decreased emotional investment at a time when relationships will be affected be death.


Activity theory of aging-

Those who age most successfully are those who maintain the levels of social interaction they experienced in middle adulthood. Late adulthood should be a continuation of the activities in which people participated during earlier life.


Elizabeth Kubler Ross-

Spent her career studying tragedy, death, and grief. She developed the five stages of grief that all people experience. The stages are denial, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance, and they occur in no particular order.


Other Important Vocabulary

Imprinting-

The process by which animals form attachments in early life.


Theory of mind-

People’s ideas about their own and other’s mental states


Critical period-

An optimal period after birth where exposure to certain stimuli produces proper development.


Autism-

A disorder that appears in childhood marked by deficient communication, social interaction, and understanding of other’s state of minds.


Habituation-

Decreasing responsiveness with repeated stimulation


Maturation-

Biological growth processes that enable changes in behavior, unaffected by experience.


Cross sectional study-

A study where people of different ages are compared


Social clock-

The culturally preferred timing of social events like marriage, parenthood, and retirement.


Longitudinal study-

A study in which the same people are restudied over a long period


Emerging adulthood-

A period from late teens to adulthood that bridges the gap from adolescence to independence


Intimacy-

The ability to form close loving relationships


Social identity-

The we aspect of self-concept


Menopause-

The natural cessation of menstruation


Menarche-

A woman’s first menstruation


Basic trust-

A sense that the world is predictable and trustworthy


Role-

A set of expectations about a social position that defines how those in the position should behave.


Aggression-

Physical or verbal behavior intended to hurt someone


Temperament-

A person’s characteristic emotional reactivity and intensity


Identity-

Our sense of self


Primary sex characteristics-

Body structures like ovaries, testes, and genitalia that make reproduction possible


Secondary sex characteristics-

Nonreproduced characteristics like breasts, hips, voice quality, and body hair


Puberty-

The period of sexual maturation, during which a person becomes capable of reproduction.


Personal space-

The area around a person they consider psychologically theirs.


Placenta-

The structure that provides oxygen and nutrients to a baby while removing waste products from its blood. Where the wall of the uterus and baby is connected through the umbilical cord.


Dementia-

A group of symptoms that affect memory, thinking, and social abilities.


Pruning-

Beginning after puberty, the process of reducing excess neural connections in the brain’s association areas.
