Lesson 3.2 Vision

Parts of the eye-

Cornea is the outside covering of the eye.


The pupil is a hole in the eye that allows light to enter.


The iris is the colored ring that controls how much light enters.


Lens focuses light onto the retina and flips the image upside down to project for the retina


Retina is where transduction occurs, where the organ translates stimulus into a neural message.


Fovea is where most of the cones are and the point of focus.


Blind spot is where the optic nerve leaves the eye


Hue-

Allows for color, the dimension of color determined by wavelength of light.


Wavelength-

The distance from the peak of one wave to the peak of the next. Short wavelengths have a higher frequency with blue colors and high-pitched sounds. Long wavelengths have red colors and lower pitches.


Intensity/brightness-

The amount of energy in a wave is determined by amplitude, which is related to perceived brightness. High amplitudes mean bright and loud, small amplitude means dull and soft. As intensity changes, colors will vary from dark to light.


Nearsightedness-

A condition where nearby objects are more closely seen because the eye is elongated, and the image focuses before it hits the retina.


Farsightedness-

A condition where far objects are more closely seen because the eye is shortened, and images focus after hitting the retina.


Retina-

The light sensitive inner surface of the eye that contains rods and cones, and other neurons including bipolar and ganglion cells, that processes visual info. Images hit the back of the retina where the cones and rods are, working their way forward to bipolar and ganglion cells. Cones allow for color to be perceived while rods allow for vision in the dark since they are light sensitive. The axons of the ganglion cells send the information out, creating the optic nerve.


Fovea-

The central point of the retina where all the cones are clustered. Since most cones are clustered here, periphery vision has little color. Rods make up peripheral vision since they are on the periphery of the retina. Cones donâ€™t exist on the furthest periphery. The pupil dilates to allow for more light to be exposed and for more rods.


Rods vs Cones-

There are 6 million cones mainly in the fovea. They are color sensitive with low sensitivity in dim light and are good at detecting details. Each cone has its own bipolar cell, allowing for more detailed and focused images.


There are 120 million rods in the periphery of the eye. They are not color sensitive but have high sensitivity in low light and are bad at detecting detail. Every 4 rods share a bipolar cell, creating distorted and less detailed images.


Bipolar cells-

Receive messages from photoreceptors and transmit them to ganglion cells.


Blind spot-

Where no visual receptors are because of the info leaving the eye.


Feature detection-

Nerve cells in the visual cortex that respond to features like edges, angle, length, and movement. There are some detectors that work best on the human face.


Visual information processing-

The processing of several aspects of the stimulus simultaneously, also known as parallel processing. The brain divides it into subdivisions like color, depth, form, and movement.


Visual process-

A scene is displayed, and retinal processing occurs (rods and cones to bipolar cells to ganglion cells). The information is sent to the thalamus where it is then sent to the visual cortex. Feature detection responds to the stimuli and detects for features. The information goes to associated areas where abstraction (higher level cells respond to information from feature detection cells) occurs. Then the info goes to the memory where the brain matches the image with stored images in the memory.


Trichromatic theory-

Also known as the young Helmholtz theory, suggests that the retina is sensitive to red, green, and blue colors.


Color deficiency/blindness-

A genetic disorder that prevents people from discriminating between colors due to a deficient cone. Red/green blindness is the most common. Complete color deficiency is rare, but it does exist.


Opponent process theory-

Hering proposed that we process 4 primary colors opposed in red-green, blue-yellow, and black-white.


Color constancy-

The color of an object will remain consistent regardless of lighting and wavelength.
