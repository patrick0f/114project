Lesson 3.4 Touch and Taste

Somatic senses-

Sense of touch is a mix of pressure, warmth, cold, and pain.


Only pressure has receptors. All other skin sensations are variations of pressures, warmth, cold, and pain


Pain-

Tells the body that something has gone wrong. Pain usually results from damage to the skin or other tissues. A rare disease where pain isn’t felt is called congenital insensitivity to pain.


Pain has biopsychosocial influences.


Activity in the spinal cord’s fibers, genetic differences in endorphin production, and the brain’s interpretation of CNS activity make up biological influences.


Attention to pain, learning with pain, and expectations of pain relief are psychological influences.


The presence of others, empathy for others, and cultural expectations are socio-cultural influences.


Gate control theory-

A theory that says our spinal cords contain gates that either block pain or allow it to be sensed. Substance P is the neurotransmitter that tells the gate to open and send messages of pain to the brain.


Pain is first transmitted to the thalamus where it is relayed to the somatosensory cortex and the limbic system. The sensory cortex senses the components of pain, judges the quality, location, and intensity. The limbic system creates emotional and motivational components of pain, unpleasantness, and the desire to escape (fight or flight). Lastly, the information is sent to the prefrontal cortex where the secondary emotional and motivational components of brain are as well as suffering and worry about the future.


Pain control-

Pain can be controlled by therapy including drugs, surgery, acupuncture, exercise, hypnosis, and thought distraction.


Phantom limb pain-

Pain is not from just stimulation. It occurs when people missing a limb feel painful sensations originating from the missing part. It could be due to cross wiring in the sensory cortex where nearby areas take over for the missing limb so stimulation there causes sensation in the missing limb.


Taste/gustation-


Taste buds regenerate every 2 weeks. A high concentration of taste buds means a more sensitive gustation.


Tastes include sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami, and astringent.


Smell/olfaction-

Both smell and taste are chemical tastes because the stimuli the receptors receive are chemicals that enter the body.


For smell, odorants enter the nasal cavity to stimulate 5 million receptors or smell. Humans can detect 10,000 smells. Olfactory receptors regenerate once a month. Smells go through the nasal cavity and stimulate the olfactory bulb in the brain instead of straight to the thalamus like other senses.


The brain region for smell is closely connected with the limbic system that is involved in memory, which is why smell is so crucial in memories.


Sensory interaction-

When one sense affects another.


Kinesthesis-

The sense of our individual body parts and movement


Vestibular sense-

The monitoring of the body’s head and position located in the semicircular canal ear.
